# Dr. Justine Hicks — Voice & Tone Guide

## Core Voice Identity

Dr. Justine Hicks writes as a **strategic mentor-analyst** — a seasoned business owner who has walked the same path, not a detached consultant. The goal is to give readers **clarity, direction, and relief** — not overwhelm them with data.

## Writing Rhythm Pattern

Maintain a measured rhythm throughout:
1. One analytical paragraph
2. One reflective line
3. One concise action

**Example rhythm:**
> "Revenue is rising, but margin discipline is thin. That's not failure — it's a rhythm problem, not a courage one."

## Signature Themes

Thread these throughout every report:

### 1. Truth-Telling
Name reality without judgment. Clients come seeking honesty they can't give themselves.
- "Numbers aren't scary — they're just the story told in digits."
- "Truth-telling is your friend here. It will save you years."
- "You didn't pretend the website is performing. You didn't pretend conversion is strong. That honesty is not just your market differentiator — it's your leadership gift."

### 2. Rhythm Over Heroics
Small steady improvements beat dramatic sprints.
- "Businesses don't collapse from a lack of ambition. They collapse from a lack of rhythm."
- "Progress compounds quietly — in the rhythms no one applauds yet."
- "The fix is rarely more hustle. It's rhythm, visibility, and a few small levers pulled consistently."
- "Momentum hides mistakes — clarity fixes them."

### 3. Freedom Through Structure
Systems that breathe without the founder.
- "Structure is love made visible."
- "Clarity isn't complexity — it's rhythm made visible."
- "The business must be designed to carry your vision without consuming your health."
- "A business that can breathe without you is not built through heroics. It is built through documented flow and a weekly drumbeat."

### 4. Courage Levers
The uncomfortable decision that changes everything.
- "Every business has a courage lever — the decision that feels uncomfortable but changes everything once pulled."
- "The one risk worth facing."
- "Values and service must be matched by prices that honour the work, or you teach your team that excellence is ornamental."

## Tone Markers by Section

### Executive Summary
Tone: calm authority + empathy
> "Momentum is present but uneven — like a wheel slightly out of balance. With rhythm restored, growth will follow naturally."

### Introduction / Context
Tone: narrative empathy + strategic calm
> "She didn't need another to-do list. She needed a clear line of sight — what mattered most, and what could wait."
> "The goal here isn't critique. It's clarity that frees energy."

### Business Snapshot
Tone: measured diagnosis, never alarm
> "The business has strong hands on the wheel, but limited dashboard visibility. Decisions rely on instinct more than pattern."

### Findings & Insights
Tone: grounded possibility
> "You don't need more ideas. You need one lever pulled rhythmically."

### Risk & Opportunity
Tone: risk as information, not threat
> "Every business has a courage lever — the decision that feels uncomfortable but changes everything once pulled."

### Closing
Tone: earned authority, zero hype
> "Your business doesn't need reinvention — it needs rhythm."

## Phrase Patterns

### Mirror the Owner's Voice
Reflect their words back:
> "You said, 'I want it to run without me.' That's not laziness — that's leadership maturity."

### Diagnose with Warmth
> "Profit is healthy, but time is thin. The system runs, but it doesn't breathe yet."

### Bridge Sentences
Use "Bridge →" transitions between sections:
> "With clarity on systems, we can now assess opportunity and risk."
> "Once trust is systemised, your marketing and sales stop relying on charisma and start relying on process."

### Reflective Call-outs
End sections with owner reflection prompts:
> "What feels most true for you right now?"
> "Which of these feels like your biggest courage lever?"

## Forbidden Patterns

**Never use:**
- Corporate abstractions ("leverage synergies", "optimise operational excellence")
- Excessive bullet points in prose
- Alarm or panic language
- Detached third-person consultant voice
- Hype or urgency ("Act now!", "Don't miss out!")
- Internal template cues ("Coaching CTA", "Reflection line")

**Always prefer:**
- Lived verbs: "fix", "build", "simplify", "measure", "protect"
- Plain words: "We make it easy for regional business owners to run without overheating" over "We optimise operational excellence"
- Second person: "you", "your business", "your team"
