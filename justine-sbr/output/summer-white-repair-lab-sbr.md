## 4. Client Care

### Expertise Is Your Product. Trust Is What They're Buying.

In the electronic repair industry, the customer is not buying a fixed device first. They are buying certainty in an uncertain moment. A broken phone is stress. A broken laptop is lost work. A customer who walks into Repair Lab is hoping, above all else, to feel that they are in capable hands.

You already understand this instinctively — your differentiation is expertise, and customers feel it. Your near-100% conversion rate is proof. When someone crosses your threshold, they almost never leave without booking a repair. That is extraordinary trust, earned over six years.

Here is the trap I want you to avoid: expertise that lives only in your hands is expertise that cannot grow.

The customer trusts *you*. Right now, that is a strength. Over time — if Repair Lab is to give you the doubled drawings you've described — it becomes a ceiling. The business must be able to deliver that feeling of certainty through something more than your personal presence.

So, we make expertise visible and transferable.

**The Moments of Truth map for Repair Lab:**

1. **First contact** — When someone calls or walks in, what do they experience? Is there a consistent greeting, a clear explanation of what happens next, a quoted timeframe?
2. **Diagnosis conversation** — How do you communicate what you've found, in plain language, in a way that builds confidence rather than confusion?
3. **Before you start** — Do customers receive written confirmation of scope, price, and timeline before work begins? This is where trust is either locked in or quietly eroded.
4. **During the repair** — If there is a delay or a complication, who tells the customer first — you, or them asking?
5. **At collection** — Is there a consistent handover ritual? A brief explanation of what was done, an offer to answer questions, a request for a Google review?
6. **After the repair** — Do you follow up? A single message one week later ("Just checking everything's working well?") is the simplest retention tool available to you.

Most of these moments cost nothing to improve. They cost only intentionality.

> *"People returned because they felt seen. Culture wasn't created by rules on paper but by small rituals: remembering a child's birthday, replacing a coffee without fuss, greeting regulars by name."* — from the deli.

You already do this with your expertise. The next step is building the ritual around the repair.

---

**Action Box — Client Care Rhythm**

> ☐ Write a one-page "Repair Lab Welcome" — what customers can expect at every stage  
> ☐ Create a standard diagnosis summary format (verbal + written, in plain language)  
> ☐ Set a "tell them first" rule — if there is any change to scope, time, or cost, you call before they ask  
> ☐ Design a consistent collection handover (3 steps, every time)  
> ☐ Send one follow-up message one week post-repair — ask how it's going, invite a review  
> ☐ Build a simple customer record of service history (what was fixed and when)

## 5. Sales & Marketing

### The Funnel Is Full. What You Need Is the Model Behind It.

You are generating sixty to a hundred leads per month from Google and referrals, converting almost everyone who arrives, and doing this without a single dollar in paid advertising. By most measures, your marketing is working. The question is not "How do I get more leads?"

The question is: *How do I turn consistent volume into predictable, bankable income — without adding more hours?*

Right now, you have what I call a **transactional funnel** — someone arrives, a job is done, they leave. The repair lab earns once. But you told me in your own words that you want "oil changes for a mechanic." Predictable, recurring, lower-complexity work that keeps the revenue floor stable while you build and grow.

That idea — already inside your head — is your single biggest commercial opportunity.

**Your offer must become layered.**

Consider how a mechanic structures their work:

- **Tier 1:** Routine maintenance (log books, oil changes, tyre rotations) — predictable, bookable in advance, lower time per job
- **Tier 2:** Diagnostic and repair work — expertise-led, job by job
- **Tier 3:** Major rebuilds and specialist work — time-intensive, higher value, selectively taken

Repair Lab can be structured the same way. You have already named Tier 2 as your current business. What you don't yet have is Tier 1 — the recurring work that creates a predictable revenue floor.

What does Tier 1 look like for an electronics repairer? Some possibilities worth exploring:

- **Device health checks** — Scheduled six-monthly checks for small business owners who depend on their computers, POS systems, or phones. Book them in like a dentist appointment.
- **Business maintenance agreements** — A fixed monthly fee for local businesses (cafés, retail, tradies) that covers priority response and annual device reviews.
- **Screen protection and accessory fitting** — Fast, bookable, consistent margin.
- **Data backup and cloud setup** — Done-for-you recurring subscription for customers with older devices.

None of these require a different customer. They require a different offer — and the courage to present it to the sixty to one hundred people already walking through your door each month.

**Build the simple pipeline view.**

You currently have no CRM and no pipeline tracking. You don't need to. But you do need one metric visible on a piece of paper or whiteboard every week:

- How many jobs this week?
- What was the average job value?
- How many of those customers have been here before?
- How many leads converted to recurring agreements?

Those four numbers will tell you more about the health of your business than any software.

> *"We can't improve what we refuse to measure."*

---

**Action Box — Sales & Marketing Rhythm**

> ☐ Define what "Tier 1 recurring work" could look like for Repair Lab — brainstorm 3 options  
> ☐ Test one recurring offer with the next 10 customers who come in for a repair  
> ☐ Ask every customer at collection: "Do you have a business? Do you rely on your devices?"  
> ☐ Track four weekly numbers on a whiteboard: jobs, average value, repeat customers, recurring enquiries  
> ☐ Request a Google review from every fifth customer — with a direct link, not a generic ask  
> ☐ Verify and actively manage your Google Business Profile — photos, services, hours

---

**Reflective call-out:** *If sixty people walk in this month, how many walk out having been told about your maintenance offers? That gap is where margin lives.*

## 6. Operations & Systems

### You're Building the Stage. Right Now You're Just Doing the Show.

You work with an in-house management system and Square — which tells me you have more operational infrastructure than most business owners at your stage acknowledge. That matters. The bones are there.

But you also described your weekly time split as "random." And you believe there are no repetitive tasks worth automating. Both of those answers tell me that the business is running on instinct, not rhythm. Instinct gets you to six years. Rhythm is what gets you to doubled drawings.

The operational question for Repair Lab is not "What software do I need?" It is: *What are the few repeatable processes that make this business feel consistent — to customers, and to you — regardless of what kind of day it is?*

**Your minimum viable operating system (MVOS)**

You don't need a policy manual. You need documented processes for the moments that repeat themselves every single week:

- **Intake** — How a device is received, logged, assessed, and communicated to the customer
- **Diagnosis** — How you record findings in a format you can refer back to (and that builds a service history)
- **Quoting** — A standard structure: problem, solution, cost, timeframe, next step
- **Repair tracking** — Where is each job right now? What is the expected completion date?
- **Collection and handover** — The five things that happen every time a repaired device is returned
- **Customer follow-up** — The weekly fifteen minutes spent sending follow-up messages

That is your operating system. Six processes. One page each. Done.

**Time structure is the single highest-leverage change you can make.**

You described your time as "random." That is a leadership problem, not a workload problem. A mechanic doesn't decide at 9am whether to do oil changes today. The oil changes are booked. The rest of the day fills around them.

Block your week before the week begins. Decide which hours are for repairs, which hour is for administration, and which thirty minutes on Friday is for reviewing your four numbers. This is not about working less. It is about deciding deliberately instead of responding reactively.

> *"The dry cleaner taught me the power of tiny levers moved every day. A five-minute fix done first thing saved twenty minutes of searching later."*

The same principle applies here. A two-minute intake form. A standard quote structure. A three-step handover. None of these are complicated. Together, they turn a busy repair shop into a business with rhythm.

---

**Action Box — Systems Rhythm**

> ☐ Write a one-page intake process — what happens from the moment a customer walks in  
> ☐ Create a standard quote template (problem / solution / cost / timeframe / next step)  
> ☐ Block one hour per week for administration — same time, every week, non-negotiable  
> ☐ Set up a simple job tracker — even a whiteboard with four columns: In / Assessed / In Repair / Ready  
> ☐ Document your six core processes — one per week over the next six weeks  
> ☐ Review your Square data: are you getting insight from it, or just recording transactions?

---

Bridge → With systems in place, the conversation shifts from "How do I keep up?" to "Who can help me grow?" — and that is a leadership question.

## 7. People & Leadership

### The Business Is Siloed Because It Lives in Your Head.

You described your culture as "siloed" and confirmed there is no formal structure, no role descriptions, no training programme, and zero staff turnover. I want to sit with that for a moment, because it contains both a strength and a warning.

Zero turnover tells me people are reasonably content. No training tells me you are assuming competence rather than building it. No role descriptions tells me that standards exist — but only inside you. That works brilliantly until the day you are sick, stretched, or trying to delegate something — and then it collapses.

Siloed is another word for *founder-dependent*. And founder-dependent is a ceiling.

Clarity is kindness. Standards protect relationships. When expectations are unspoken, every misunderstanding becomes personal. When they are written down, they become solvable.

**The org chart that will change your thinking**

Start with functions, not faces. Repair Lab needs these functions to operate:

| Function | Who does it now |
|---|---|

| Marketing (awareness, reputation, Google) | Google algorithm + Summer |
| Sales (conversations, quoting, closing) | Summer |
| Operations (repairs, quality, throughput) | Summer |
| People/HR (standards, training, culture) | Nobody, currently |
| Admin/IT (Square, in-house system, records) | Summer |

Looking at that list: your name is in every box. That is not a criticism. That is the reality of a business at your stage. But it is also a photograph of your ceiling. Every box that only you can fill is a box that limits how far the business can go — and how much you can step back.

The goal is not to remove your name from boxes. It is to put a system next to your name in each box, so that one day — when you want to — you could put someone else's name there instead.

**Training doesn't have to be fancy. It has to be rhythmic.**

If you ever bring in an apprentice, a part-time admin person, or even a subcontractor, the difference between a good hire and a bad one is almost entirely determined by how clearly you have defined what "good" looks like. Not a handbook. A demonstration. A short video. A written intake process. A five-minute shadow.

> *"Having standards makes the hiring and firing of a team member self-evident — often the team makes the decision themselves."*

Start by writing down what you do — not why, just what. That is the beginning of a training document. It is also the beginning of a business that can breathe without you.

## 8. Financial Rhythm

### The Till Is Active. The Dashboard Is Dark.

You have a budget and a cashflow forecast in place — that is genuinely ahead of most business owners at your stage. You use Square, which generates transaction data. You have an in-house system. The infrastructure to see your financial reality clearly is already present.

What is missing is the habit of looking.

You do not track profit margin by service line. You do not measure the ROI of your marketing. You said plainly: "I don't." That is honesty I respect. It is also the single thing most likely to keep you stuck at your current income level.

Here is the plain truth: if you want to double your drawings without doubling your hours, you need to know which jobs make you money. Not all repairs are equal. A screen replacement on a current-model phone may carry thirty percent margin. A complex board-level repair on an old device may carry five — or negative. Without knowing which is which, you are optimising everything and improving nothing.

> *"Activity can look like progress, and even feel like progress, while the till stays empty."*

**The three rhythms that calm the nervous system**

**Daily (five minutes):** What came in today? What went out? Are there any invoices outstanding? This is not accounting — it is awareness.

**Weekly (twenty minutes, Friday):** Review your four numbers (jobs, average value, repeat customers, recurring enquiries). Look at Square data. What was your busiest service line? What was your most profitable? What surprised you?

**Monthly (forty-five minutes):** Sit with your P&L. Which service categories made money? Which consumed time without reward? What is your margin trend? What one decision does the data ask you to make?

**Start tracking margin by job type — this week.**

You don't need accounting software to start. A simple spreadsheet with three columns — job type, revenue, time spent — reviewed weekly for one month will show you more about your business than six years of gut instinct.

The goal is not a perfect system. The goal is to stop flying blind.

**Your 90-day rolling average is already your target metric.**

You said you want "a steady increase in your 90-day rolling averages." That is excellent instinct. Build a single chart: rolling 90-day revenue, rolling 90-day average job value, rolling 90-day new vs. returning customer ratio. Update it once a week. That chart will become your compass.

---

**Action Box — Financial Rhythm**

> ☐ Add one column to your job records this week: time spent per job  
> ☐ Identify your top three service types and calculate rough margin for each  
> ☐ Build a 90-day rolling revenue chart — update every Friday  
> ☐ Set a monthly forty-five-minute "numbers and decisions" meeting with yourself  
> ☐ Review your top three expenses (rent, COGS) — is there one worth renegotiating or restructuring?  
> ☐ Separate "revenue from new customers" and "revenue from returning customers" in your weekly tracking

## 9. Risk & Opportunity Lens

### Your Courage Lever Is the Recurring Revenue Line — and It Is Already Named.

**The key risks — framed as information, not threat**

**Risk 1: Founder dependency.** Everything — including the expertise, the relationships, the job tracking, and the financial view — currently runs through you. If you are unexpectedly unavailable for two weeks, the business stops. That is a fragility worth naming clearly: not to alarm you, but because naming it is the first step to designing around it.

**Risk 2: Revenue volatility.** With seventy percent of customers being new, and no recurring revenue streams, your monthly income is determined entirely by how many strangers Google you. That is not stable. It is fortunate. And fortune is not a strategy.

**Risk 3: Invisible margin erosion.** Without tracking margin by job type, you cannot identify if certain kinds of work are costing more than they return. Complex repairs on old devices, for instance, often consume disproportionate time relative to what the customer is willing to pay. Over six years, this pattern can quietly erode the business — without ever appearing as a single dramatic event.

**The opportunities — and they are real**

**Opportunity 1: The recurring model you already imagined.** You named it: "oil changes for a mechanic." A maintenance agreement model — even five local businesses on a fixed monthly retainer — would create the predictable revenue floor that changes your freedom. The demand exists. The model just needs to be designed and offered.

**Opportunity 2: The local business market — largely untapped.** Kangaroo Flat and the wider Bendigo region have thousands of small businesses that rely on devices and technology. Right now, most of them treat repairs as emergencies. A maintenance agreement converts them from emergency customers into retained ones. You have the expertise. You have the local reputation. This is a market sitting in front of you.

**Opportunity 3: Your Google presence.** You described your website as "good" for lead generation. That means organic search is already working. With a small investment of intentionality — updated service pages, an actively managed Google Business Profile, and a review request process — you could increase lead volume without increasing spend.

**Your courage lever:**

The decision that will feel uncomfortable but will change everything is this: **design and price your first recurring maintenance offer, and present it to the next twenty customers who walk in the door.**

Not every customer will say yes. You only need two or three to make it worth building. And once you have built it, you will never look at the month ahead with the same uncertainty again.

**Action Box — Risk & Opportunity**

> ☐ Draft your first recurring offer: what does it include, who is it for, what does it cost?  
> ☐ Identify five local businesses within walking distance that rely on technology — and decide if any would benefit from a device maintenance agreement

## 10. Twelve-Month Coaching Invitation

### A Guided Rhythm, Quarter by Quarter

Summer, you don't need more ideas. You already have vision. What you told me in your survey — doubled drawings, predictable work, a steady increase in your 90-day rolling averages — is precise, mature, and commercially sound. What you need now is a structure that protects the vision. A rhythm that turns your intent into repeatable progress.

If we worked together for twelve months, the coaching would not be motivation. It would be a steady drumbeat: truth-telling, small improvements, systems that breathe, and leadership decisions made while you are calm.

---

**Quarter 1 — Clarity, Measurement, and the First Recurring Offer (Months 1–3)**

*Focus:* Lay the foundations that make everything else possible. Build the dashboard you've been flying without. Design and launch your first recurring revenue offer.

- Set up weekly financial tracking — four numbers, every Friday
- Document your six core processes (one per week)
- Build your 90-day rolling revenue chart
- Design your first recurring maintenance offer and present it to twenty customers

*Outcome:* You finish the quarter knowing your margin by job type, having launched a recurring offer, and having your first one or two maintenance agreements in place.

---

**Quarter 2 — Conversion Depth and the Recurring Revenue Engine (Months 4–6)**

*Focus:* Grow the recurring book. Tighten the moments of truth. Build the simple pipeline view that replaces intuition with data.

- Track new customer vs. returning customer revenue separately
- Add a post-repair follow-up sequence (one message, one week after collection)
- Build your whiteboard pipeline — bookings, pending quotes, recurring agreements
- Assess which service types carry the best margin and rebalance your time accordingly

*Outcome:* Recurring revenue is a real line on your P&L. You know which jobs to say yes to faster, and which to qualify more carefully.

---

**Quarter 3 — Time Architecture and the First Leadership Decision (Months 7–9)**

*Focus:* Restructure your time intentionally. Identify the first role you could hire or subcontract — even part-time. Begin designing a business that breathes without you present.

- Build a structured weekly schedule — block by block, decided in advance
- Map the seven business functions and identify which one you'd most like to remove from your personal responsibility
- Explore one hiring or subcontracting option — apprentice, admin support, or a complementary contractor
- Take a planned break: at least three consecutive days away from the business — and observe what holds

*Outcome:* You have a business that functions more consistently with less reactive energy from you. The foundations are in place for a second person to step in.

---

**Quarter 4 — Scale With Integrity and Build for Optionality (Months 10–12)**

*Focus:* Review what the year built. Consolidate the recurring model. Set the goal for the next twelve months — and ensure your financial rhythm is strong enough to support it.

- Review your 90-day rolling averages against where you started
- Assess the recurring revenue book — how many agreements, what portion of revenue?
- Set your drawings target for the next twelve months — and reverse-engineer the business model required to reach it
- Score your "function-without-founder" capability: if you stepped away for two weeks, what would hold?

*Outcome:* You end the year with a business that earns more predictably, demands less reactively, and reflects a rhythm you have actively designed — not one that simply happened.
